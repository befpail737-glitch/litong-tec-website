Top 7 Common Failure Modes in IGBT Modules: An FAE's Perspective

By John Doe, FAE | Published on: August 18, 2025 | Category: Failure Analysis

Understanding why IGBTs fail is the first step to designing a robust and reliable system. Here are some of the most common failure modes we encounter in the field and how to mitigate them.

1. Short-Circuit Failure

Cause: A low-impedance path across the DC link, causing massive current to flow through the IGBT. Prevention: A fast-acting gate driver with DESAT (desaturation) protection is essential. It must detect the event and safely shut down the IGBT within its rated Short-Circuit Withstand Time (typically 5-10Âµs).

2. Overheating / Thermal Runaway

Cause: Inadequate cooling, incorrect mounting, or exceeding the module's power dissipation limits. Prevention: Perform careful thermal calculations. Ensure your heatsink is correctly sized, use the recommended type and thickness of thermal paste, and apply the correct mounting torque to the module's screws.

3. Over-Voltage Failure

Cause: Voltage overshoot during turn-off, caused by parasitic inductance in the DC busbar. Prevention: Minimize the inductance between the DC-link capacitor and the IGBT module. Use laminated busbars and select a high-quality, low-ESL capacitor.

4. Gate Oxide Breakdown

Cause: Applying excessive voltage to the gate terminal (Vge), often from ESD or a faulty gate driver. Prevention: Use proper ESD handling procedures. Ensure your gate driver voltage is well-regulated and clamped within the datasheet limits.
