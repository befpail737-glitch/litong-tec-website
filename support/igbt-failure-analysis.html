Top 7 Common Failure Modes in IGBT Modules: An FAE's Perspective

By LiTong FAE Team | Published on: August 18, 2025 | Category: Failure Analysis

Understanding why IGBTs fail is the first step to designing a robust and reliable system. Here are some of the most common failure modes we encounter in the field and how to mitigate them.

1. Short-Circuit Failure

Cause: A low-impedance path across the DC link, causing massive current to flow through the IGBT.

Prevention: A fast-acting gate driver with DESAT (desaturation) protection is essential. It must detect the event and safely shut down the IGBT within its rated Short-Circuit Withstand Time (typically 5-10Âµs). Modules like the T-IGBT-H3300 have enhanced short-circuit ruggedness.

2. Overheating / Thermal Runaway

Cause: Inadequate cooling, incorrect mounting, or exceeding the module's power dissipation limits.

Prevention: Perform careful thermal calculations. Ensure your heatsink is correctly sized, use the recommended type and thickness of thermal paste, and apply the correct mounting torque to the module's screws.

3. Over-Voltage Failure

Cause: Voltage overshoot during turn-off, caused by parasitic inductance in the DC busbar.

Prevention: Minimize the inductance between the DC-link capacitor and the IGBT module. Use laminated busbars and select a high-quality, low-ESL capacitor.

4. Gate Oxide Breakdown

Cause: Applying excessive voltage to the gate terminal (Vge), often from ESD or a faulty gate driver.

Prevention: Use proper ESD handling procedures. Ensure your gate driver voltage is well-regulated and clamped within the datasheet limits.

5. Miller Turn-On (dV/dt Induced Turn-On)

Cause: Rapid voltage change across the collector-emitter during turn-off can induce current in the gate-emitter loop, potentially turning the IGBT on unintentionally.

Prevention: Use a negative gate bias (-5V to -15V) to provide gate-emitter margin. Minimize gate loop inductance. Consider active Miller clamps for critical applications.

6. Excessive Cycling Fatigue

Cause: Repeated thermal cycling during normal operation causes mechanical stress and eventual failure at bond wires or solder joints.

Prevention: Select modules with proven power cycling capability like the T-IGBT-W1700. Optimize thermal design to minimize temperature swings. Use appropriate lifetime prediction models.

7. Moisture/Corrosion Related Failures

Cause: Exposure to humidity, salt spray, or corrosive environments can lead to degradation of electrical connections and insulation.

Prevention: Ensure proper IP ratings for enclosures. Use conformal coating where appropriate. Select modules with robust environmental ratings. Follow proper storage and handling procedures.

Conclusion

By understanding these common failure modes and implementing appropriate design practices, you can significantly improve the reliability and lifespan of your IGBT-based systems.
